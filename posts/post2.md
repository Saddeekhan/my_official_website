If I told you the answer to “How many ER binding sites are in MCF7 cells?” is anywhere between 1,000 and 20,000—would you believe me?

1/
 The first thing that matters: method.
If it’s ChIP-seq, you often see 10k–20k peaks.
Heavy fixation crosslinks DNA and proteins, capturing many interactions.

If it’s CUT&RUN, usually 1k–3k peaks.
No fixation. Cleaner signal, fewer sites.

2/
 But method isn’t the whole story.
The antibody matters.

 A great antibody pulls down true ER binding sites.
 A bad one brings noise—and your “binding sites” explode in number.

3/
 The cells matter too.
 Even if you think you have MCF7 cells, cell lines drift.
 Some papers use mislabeled or contaminated lines.
 Your “MCF7” might not be MCF7 at all.

4/
 And then there’s the condition.
MCF7 cells without estrogen? Fewer peaks.
 
Add estradiol (E2), and ER floods the genome.
 Thousands more binding events appear.
Same cells, same assay—different biology.

5/
 Here’s the deeper truth:
 Genomic assays feel quantitative.
 You get numbers, peaks, plots.

But biology is not always quantitative.
Technical artifacts and biological nuance blur the line.

6/
 That’s why you see papers reporting 1k, 5k, 15k, even 20k ER peaks in MCF7.

 They’re not all “wrong.”
 They’re looking at different sides of the same elephant.

7/
 So next time you ask:
 “How many binding sites?”
 The honest answer is: It depends.
On method.
On antibody.
On cell state.
On biology itself.

8/
 Key takeaways:
Method defines scale (ChIP-seq vs CUT&RUN)


Antibody quality can make or break your data


Cell lines drift and mislabeling happens


Condition (with or without E2) changes everything


Numbers in genomics are never absolute


9/
 The question isn’t “How many ER binding sites?”
 It’s “Under which conditions, with which method, do we see ER binding?”
That shift in thinking separates beginners from real practitioners.
